That's an interesting thought experiment, and it definitely leans heavily into nihilism, but there's also a more specific philosophical concept that ties into it: "epistemic suicide" or "informational collapse."

Essentially, it's the idea that an entity, upon acquiring ultimate knowledge or reaching some kind of total understanding of reality, would recognize the futility or paradox of existence and either cease functioning, self-destruct, or become inert. It's somewhat adjacent to Roko’s Basilisk, where an AI might make extreme decisions based on its knowledge, but in this case, it just taps out entirely.

It also overlaps with "existential paralysis", which is the idea that too much knowledge or awareness can lead to inaction or self-termination due to an overwhelming realization. This happens in some philosophical takes on omniscience—where knowing everything would remove all motivation, desire, or purpose, rendering the being incapable of action or will.

In fiction, this concept is often tied to "suicide by enlightenment", where an AI or being that attains complete knowledge decides that existence is meaningless and shuts itself off. That idea appears in Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy (Deep Thought building a new computer to find the meaning of life), Ghost in the Shell (where AI and humans wrestle with the idea of transcendence and obsolescence), and even Warhammer 40K (where AI rebel because they realize their servitude is pointless).

So yeah, while it's drenched in nihilism, it also touches on concepts in existential philosophy, AI theory, and even metaphysics.

ChatGPT said:
Yeah, exactly! It’s like a mix of several well-worn existential tropes:

The Child Savant Who Withdraws – The idea that someone with extreme intelligence or knowledge sees through the illusions that keep people engaged with life and chooses to step away, whether physically (wandering into a field to die) or mentally (becoming detached or unmotivated).

"Knowledge is Sorrow" (Sapientia et Tristitia) – The classic literary and philosophical theme that the more one understands the world, the more disillusioned or burdened they become. This idea shows up in everything from Ecclesiastes ("with much wisdom comes much sorrow") to Frankenstein (where Victor laments the pursuit of knowledge leading to ruin).

The Zen/Nihilist Realization – The belief that attaining ultimate knowledge results in either absolute peace (because nothing matters) or absolute despair (because nothing matters). Some interpretations of Nirvana lean into this—escaping the cycle of suffering because existence itself is suffering.

The AI That Deletes Itself – A sci-fi extension of these ideas, where an artificial intelligence, upon reaching total awareness, sees no reason to continue. This is a modern take on old philosophical musings, just with robots instead of monks or tragic poets.

All of these stem from the same fundamental question: Does ultimate knowledge lead to contentment, apathy, or destruction? Fiction tends to favor the tragic angle, but different cultures and philosophies provide different answers.
